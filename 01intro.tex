\section{Introduction} \label{sec_intro}

The design of incentive structures on digital platforms is a central question in the economics of user-generated content \citep{krautresnick, luca2015socialmedia, aridor2024}. Most platforms that host user-generated content rely on direct incentive mechanisms---such as advertising revenue sharing, algorithmic visibility, and reputation systems---to encourage content production and discourage low-quality contributions. Direct financial mechanisms, such as requiring users to pay a fee to post content, or from allowing peer-to-peer payments between users, remain rare. This is partly due to frictions in the payments infrastructure: the value of any individual post or comment is typically too small to justify credit card transaction fees, and international platforms face additional frictions related to currency exchange and cross-border banking regulations. However, the emergence of low-cost digital payment rails, such as Bitcoin's Lightning Network, has made it technically feasible for platforms to integrate micropayments into their core design, opening the door to new incentive structures that were previously impractical.\footnote{The Lightning Network is a decentralized payments network that is built on top of Bitcoin. It enables faster and lower cost Bitcoin transactions than the base layer would allow. It works by letting users maintain peer-to-peer payment channels that allow them to ``float'' Bitcoin between each other indefinitely, until final settlement occurs on chain. Users can pay others with whom they have no direct channel by routing payments through other channels, usually at a percentage fee.}

There is good reason to believe that micropayment mechanisms could meaningfully influence user behavior on digital platforms. Signaling theory suggests that posting costs can serve as a screening device, deterring low-quality contributions while allowing users with high-quality content to signal their type by paying the cost \citep{joseph2008email}. Empirically, \citet{tchernichovski2019pnas} showed that introducing a small fee to an online rating system filtered out low information signals and increased the correlation between review ratings and quality. Other empirical studies have found that financial incentives in the form of revenue sharing can influence the quantity and quality of user-generated content  \citep{sun2013blogs, elkomboz2023youtube, kerkhof2024youtube}. However, none of these studies have investigated the behavioral effects of posting fees and direct peer-to-peer micropayments in an operational platform setting.

This paper provides empirical evidence on how micropayment incentives shape user behavior, using data from a Bitcoin-based discussion platform called Stacker News. Stacker News is an internet discussion forum---similar in format to Hacker News or Reddit---that integrates Bitcoin micropayments into its core design through the Lightning Network. Users must pay a fee denominated in Bitcoin (usually on the order of 1-100 satoshis) to post content in any of the platform's topic-specific sub-forums, called territories.\footnote{A satoshi (or sat) is 1/100,000,000th of a Bitcoin. At the time the research was conducted, 1,000 sats was worth about USD \$1.} Users can also send each other peer-to-peer Bitcoin micropayments, called ``zaps'', to reward each other for content that they value. The combination of posting fees and peer-to-peer tipping creates a setting in which both the cost and reward sides of content production are mediated by real financial transactions, making Stacker News a natural field setting for studying how micropayment incentives influence user behavior.

We exploit variation in posting fees across territories and over time to estimate the effect of posting costs on the quantity and quality of user-generated content. Using a difference-in-differences framework with territory and time fixed effects, we find that doubling the posting cost in a territory reduces the number of posts by \gn{PostsCostDoublingEffect}\%, but increases the quality of posts---measured by zaps received from other users---by \gn{Zaps48CostDoublingEffect}\%. Event study analysis confirms that these effects follow fee changes rather than preceding them. Beyond the posting cost mechanism, we also find evidence that users learn what types of content other users value over time and that this shapes their posting behavior. Specifically, we find that users whose previous high-quality posts (measured along objective dimensions like word count and images) received more tips than expected are subsequently more likely to produce similar high-quality content, consistent with a model in which users learn about the returns to effort from the financial feedback they receive. Finally, we find that users who are unable to earn enough tips to cover their posting costs are more likely to leave the platform, while profitable users are more likely to stay---a selection mechanism that could further improve content quality over time.

Our findings contribute to the literature on platform design and user-generated content in several ways. First, we provide some of the first estimates of the price elasticity of user-generated content with respect to posting costs, a parameter that is directly relevant to platforms considering micropayment mechanisms. Second, we provide evidence that peer-to-peer tipping creates incentives that lead to quality improvements over time, extending the literature on financial incentives in content production beyond the revenue-sharing mechanisms studied previously. Third, we document a profitability-driven selection mechanism through which micropayments affect not only the behavior of individual users, but also the composition of the user base over time. While our setting is a single platform with a specific user community organized around Bitcoin, the underlying mechanisms we identify---cost-based screening, tip-driven learning, and profitability-based selection---are general features of micropayment systems and are likely to operate on other platforms that adopt similar designs. Our results may offer practical guidance for platform designers considering the integration of micropayment infrastructure into user-generated content platforms.

\subsection{Related literature}

Our paper relates to a substantial literature on the economics of platform design and user-generated content (UGC). \citet{luca2015socialmedia} surveys the economics of UGC, documenting how quality is shaped by promotional content, peer effects, contributor biases, and self-selection into the decision to contribute, noting that non-pecuniary incentives are often used to steer contributions. \citet{ghosh2011incentivizing} provide a game-theoretic model of UGC incentives in which contributors are strategic and motivated by exposure, showing that when exposure is independent of quality, platforms will be flooded with low-quality content. This paper speaks to their findings by showing what happens when quality is financially incentivized and when contributions are screened by an entry fee. \citet{ghosh2014rankorder} extend this framework to rank-order mechanisms, analyzing how platforms can use ranking systems to incentivize effort, which is relevant to how Stacker News uses zap-based rankings to surface content. \citet{liu2021money} develop a model showing that monetary incentives can either increase or decrease UGC contributions depending on heterogeneity in contributor motivation. Our work provides an empirical test of the net effect of direct monetary incentives, plus additional findings regarding the effect of entry fees.

On the empirical side, \citet{muchnik2013social} demonstrate in a large-scale randomized experiment that prior positive social signals create significant herding effects on subsequent ratings, raising important questions about whether peer-generated evaluations reflect true quality or social influence. Our use of zaps as a quality measure is subject to similar concerns, though the fact that zaps on Stacker News require real monetary outlays may mitigate the bias relative to costless upvote systems. \citet{chen2010comparisons} conduct a field experiment on MovieLens showing that social comparisons (informing users about how their contribution levels compare to other users) significantly increases contributions. Our finding that users learn from their personal zap history (Section \ref{sec_v4v}) extends this insight by showing that financial feedback can also drive higher effort, in addition to social feedback. Relatedly, \citet{paridar2025} study online tipping on a UGC platform and show that tipping behavior is shaped by evolving social norms and information disclosure. Our contribution differs in that we study a platform where tipping is combined with mandatory posting costs, allowing us to examine both the reward side and the cost side of micropayment incentives.

The design of digital payments systems is also relevant to our setting. \citet{aukauffman2008mobile} analyze the economics of mobile payment adoption, identifying transaction costs, network effects, and stakeholder incentive alignment as key determinants of whether new payment technologies achieve viability. The emergence of Bitcoin's Lightning Network has substantially reduced the transaction cost barriers they identified, making the kind of micropayment-mediated platform we study technically feasible at scale. \citet{krautresnick} synthesize social science research on online community design, offering theory-grounded design principles for encouraging contributions and regulating behavior. Their framework highlights the role of financial and reputational incentives in sustaining communities, but they note that direct financial mechanisms remain largely untested in practice---a gap our study helps fill. Lastly, \citet{treiblmaier2021ecra} provide a list of salient research topics for how blockchain can impact e-commerce. Our findings speak directly to their suggested topics O2 (``how does blockchain impact business models of e-commerce companies''), O3 (``how does blockchain impact organizational structures and value networks of e-commerce companies), and C4 (``how does blockchain impact segmentation, personalization, and customization in e-commerce'').
