\section{Value-for-value incentives and content quality}\label{sec_v4v}

We now consider our second hypothesis: that the types of content better rewarded by zaps will increase in prevalence over time as users learn that these kinds of content are better rewarded. As in the previous section, we first extend the model of posting behavior to account for effort and learning, then we test the empirical predictions of the model.

\paragraph{Model.} We extend the model from Section \ref{sec_pay_to_post} to account for effort. As before, opportunities to post still arrive as a Poisson process with rate $\gamma$. But before observing $(q,\epsilon)$, the user first decides how much effort to exert in crafting the post. Effort affects the distribution of post quality and intrinsic motivation, $f(q,\epsilon;e)$ (users may be more motivated to post if they've exerted a lot of effort). The cost of exerting effort is $\kappa(e)$ with $\kappa(0)=0$ and $\kappa^\prime(e)>0$.  The user's expected utility conditional on effort is:
\begin{align*}
U(e) = E_{q,\epsilon|e}\left[ \max \left\{ V(q) -c+\epsilon,0  \right\}\right] - \kappa(e)
\end{align*}
Without uncertainty, the model would be equivalent to the one presented in Section \ref{sec_pay_to_post}, just with the distribution of $(q,\epsilon)$ moderated by the optimal effort level. We therefore assume that users have uncertainty over the returns to quality. That is, let:
\begin{align}
V(q) = \exp\left(\theta q\right)  \label{eq_Vq_exp_theta_q}
\end{align}
where users have uncertainty over $\theta$. Note that this is without loss of generality because $q$ has no natural units. 

Given that users are uncertain about $\theta$, they must infer it from their received zaps relative to quality. We assume that users observe $q$. Let $z_i$ be the zaps received within a fixed time period, e.g. 48 hours, for a specific post $i$.  $z_i$ is a noisy signal proportional to $V(q_i)$, and therefore:
\begin{align}
\ln z_i = \theta + q_i + \text{noise}  \label{eq_zi}
\end{align}

Now let a user have a posting history of posts indexed by $i=1,\ldots,N$. An unbiased estimator for $\theta$ is:
\begin{align}
\hat{\theta} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \ln z_i - q_i
\end{align}
That is, the user's unbiased estimate for $\theta$ is the average of their posts' zap ``surprise'', i.e. how much additional zapping it received over and above its baseline quality level.\footnote{This assumes diffuse priors; if the user had a more precise prior, their posterior mean would still be linear in $\frac{1}{N}\sum \ln z_i - q_i$.}

We can now demonstrate that effort exertion is increasing in $\hat{\theta}$ and that, as a corollarly, the expected quality of the next post is increasing in $\hat{\theta}$.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop_v4v}
Let $i=1,\ldots,N$ index a user's previously made posts. Let $q_i$ denote the quality of those posts, let $z_i \propto V(q_i)$ be a noisy signal proportional to the present value of zaps that post $i$ receives, and let $\hat{\theta} = \frac{1}{N} \sum \ln z_i - q_i$. Furthermore, assume that $F(q | \epsilon;e)$ satisfies FOSD in $e$, conditional on $\epsilon$. Then, optimal effort is weakly increasing in $\hat{\theta}$.  
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A proof is provided in the appendix.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Evidence of learning.} Since $\hat{\theta}$ is the average of the zap surprises across previous posts, Proposition \ref{prop_v4v} suggests that we can test for the presence of learning by regressing the quality of a user's next post on the average zap surprise for all prior posts made by that user. Specifically, let $i$ index a user and let $k$ index the user's $k$th post. We would run regressions of the following form:
\begin{align}
q_{i,k+1} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \underbrace{\frac{1}{k} \sum_{j=1}^{k}\left[\ln z_{ij} - q_{ij}\right]}_{\hat{\theta}_{ik}} + \epsilon_{i,k+1} \label{eq_v4v_qreg}
\end{align}
where $z_{ij}$ is the zaps user $i$ received on their $j$th post after the first 48 hours, and $q_{ij}$ is the quality of user $i$'s $j$th post. If users are learning about the returns to effort from past zap surprises, we would expect to estimate $\beta_1 > 0$. Intuitively, identification comes from the following thought experiment: imagine two otherwise identical users, but by random chance user A has had a more positive zap history than user B for the same quality posts. User A therefore has a more optimistic view of the returns to effort than user B, and will on the subsequent post exert more effort, resulting in higher expected post quality.

To measure $q_{ij}$, we make use of equation \eqref{eq_zi}, which is the idea that the zap amount within any fixed time window is a noisy measure of $q_{ij}$. We can therefore estimate $q_{ij}$ for each post by regressing zaps on objectively measured post attributes, such as number of words, whether the post is a link post, the number of images and links in the content, etc:
\begin{align}
\ln z_{ij} = \theta + \underbrace{X_{ij}\beta}_{q_{ij}}+\varepsilon_{ij} \label{eq_zreg}
\end{align}
The model's predicted values of $X_{ij} \hat{\beta}$ can then be used as unbiased estimates of the post quality $q_{ij}$. 

Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg} reports results from regressions based on equation \eqref{eq_zreg}. Column 1 starts with three attributes: the number of words in the post, whether or not the post is a link post, and the number of images and links in the post body. As discussed earlier, link posts are posts that point to a URL. They can have empty post bodies (no words), but the author of a link post can also add text to the post body to provide context for the link. As confirmed by the results in Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg}, link posts are generally less well-rewarded on Stacker News than non-link posts. By contrast, posts with links and images \emph{in the post body} are usually better rewarded on average than posts without links and images. The presence of images and links in the post body may signal that the author has spent some time thinking about and working on the post, both to make it more attractive and engaging (images), and to make it more useful (referrals to other relevant URLs). 

Column 2 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg} adds text embeddings to the vector of post attributes. The embeddings were computed from post text using OpenAI's \texttt{text-embedding-3-small} embedding model. The embedding model computes a 1,536 dimensional embedding vector for each post that encodes the meaning of the text in such a way that two posts with similar text will have short distances between their embedding vectors. Before including these embedding vectors in the vector of attributes, we first reduce their dimensionality by performing principal components analysis (PCA) with 10 components. The PCA extracts the 10 orthogonal component vectors that explain the most variance in the original 1,536 dimensional vector. We then include the 10 principal components in the attribute vector $X_{ij}$ for the regression.\footnote{Please refer to the appendix for further methodological details.} 

Lastly, Column 3 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg} includes user fixed effects and territory fixed effects. It is appropriate to include these features in our definition of $q_{ij}$, because $q_{ij}$ is meant to capture all the predictable features of post quality, and it is reasonable to assume that users have a sense of which territories are better rewarded and a sense of the baseline quality of their own writing. 

Column 3 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg} is our preferred specification. We therefore take the cofficients as estimated in Column 3 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_zreg} (including the user and territory fixed effects), and estimate $q_{ij}$ for each post using only those attributes. We then regress $q_{i,k+1}$ on $\hat{\theta}_{ik}$, the average of past post residuals, as suggested in equation \eqref{eq_v4v_qreg}. Table \ref{tbl_v4v_qreg} shows the results of this regression. Note that user and territory fixed effects are included in the regression by default. This is necessary because these fixed effects are mechanically correlated with $q_{i,k+1}$ and because we are trying to control for two otherwise identical users who have different histories of zap surprises. In Column 2 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_qreg}, we additionally include the user's history of total prior zaps and total prior to posts, to further control for time-varying user heterogeneity. Lastly, in Column 3 we include week fixed effects to control for global time trends in the quality of posts. Global time trends could itself be due to learning: especially if users learn not just from their own posts but from the posts of other users. Controlling for global time trends therefore isolates the effect of $\beta_1$ to the individualized learning that a user experiences from their personal zap history.

Using Column 3 of Table \ref{tbl_v4v_qreg} as our preferred specification, the results show an elasticity of \gn{LearningElasticity} between subsequent post quality and the average of past zap surprises. The effect is modest: it implies that if user A has twice the average past zap surprise of user B, that user A's subsequent post will be of \gn{LearningDoublingEffect} percent higher quality, measured by expected zaps received.  Although the magnitude of the effect is small, the effect is statistically significant and robust to the inclusion of various controls, as shown in Table \ref{tbl_v4v_qreg}. 

Moreover, we note that this regression estimates learning based on individual experiences only, and partials out the effect of global learning via the week fixed effects. If we hypothesize that global learning is also present, then we should expect to see higher post quality over time, as measured by objective post attributes. And this is indeed what we see: Figure \ref{fig_v4v_q_trend} plots the average of $q_{ij}$ by week, showing that this has indeed been increasing over time. In other words, post quality---as measured by number of words and semantic features---has been increasing over time, which is evidence of global learning and quality improvement driven by value-for-value incentives.\footnote{Note that this is explicitly \emph{not} driven by changes to zap generosity over time, since $q_{ij}$ is measured based on post characteristics only.}
