\begin{abstract}
We study how financial micro-incentives shape user behavior on a digital platform that uses Bitcoin micropayments. On the platform, users must pay a small fee to post content, and can earn peer-to-peer tips from other users who value their contributions. Using variation in posting fees across the platform's sub-forums and over time, we estimate the elasticity of content quantity and content quality with respect to posting cost. We find that doubling the posting fee reduces the number of posts by \gn{PostsCostDoublingEffect}\% but increases the quality of posts by \gn{Zaps48CostDoublingEffect}\%, consistent with a signaling model in which posting costs screen for higher-quality contributions. We also find evidence consistent with users learning over time which types of content are valued by others, leading to gradual improvements in content quality. Finally, we show that users who are unable to recoup their posting costs through tips are more likely to leave the platform, while profitable users are more likely to stay. These findings provide empirical evidence on the behavioral effects of micropayment and peer-to-peer incentives in digital platforms.
\end{abstract}
